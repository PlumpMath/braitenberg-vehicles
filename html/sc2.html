Wired for Free Will



Valentino Braitenberg is a cybernetician and neuroscientist. While he was director of the Max Planck Institute of Biological Cybernetics in Germany he wrote a very fun little book called Vehicles: Experiments in Synthetic Psychology. Its 14 chapters present a series of hypothetical machines ("vehicles") that display increasingly sophisticated behavior through the use of extremely simple and biologically plausible circuits and mechanisms. By the last chapter, he has described machines that not only act alive, but also seem to exhibit concept formation, foresight, personality and free will.

Some of you may not believe that a machine can think or feel. To others it may seem obvious that complex machines can become as intelligent as humans. Either way, you will be shocked (if not offended) when I show you just how easy it is to create an artificial organism that has free will by combining a few astoundingly simple circuits in one cute little machine.

Some information on Braitenberg's vehicles is available on the web. My pages at <http://www.cs.uchicago.edu/~wiseman/vehicles/> have some information about a little vehicle simulator I've written, and you can watch quicktime movies of a couple simple machines in action. There are also some good notes on the book at <http://web.psych.ualberta.ca/~mike/Pearl_Street/Vehicles/home.html>.

For dessert, I would like to demonstrate a natural language understanding system, DPMA (Dynamic Predictive Memory Architecture), that I helped develop for NASA. It is connected to a simulation of a flying space robot called "AERCam" that's intended to float around the space shuttle using a video camera to inspect the exterior (A dumb version of AERCam just successfully flew on the last shuttle mission). DPMA accepts normal English commands and queries and can hold simple conversations.
